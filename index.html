Recent as of Sunday, November 10, 2019 - On the eleventh hour of the eleventh day of the eleventh month, 1918, Europe breathed a sigh of relief as the Great War ended. Twenty years later, an even greater conflict left the world in ruins.

November is often overlooked in the holiday season because of Christmas and the relative lack of importance of Thanksgiving. This is one of the most worrying mistakes we make as a world. November 11 represents something far more important than any other holiday does: the costs of nationalism and war.

In recent years, nationalist tendencies have flared up across both the United States and Europe. The rise of the far-right should be utterly condemned and absolutely offensive to any decent person alive. Seventy years of peace in Europe have only been possible because of international cooperation and a shared sense of remembrance of just how catastrophic that not just the world wars, but all wars, have been.

Armistice Day and Veteran's Day are the two most common names for this holiday. There exists, however, a third: Rememberance Day. A day to pause and reflect on how bad these wars were and how humans must never allow their most base instinicts to do such horrendous things ever again.

On December 12, 2019, the United Kingdom will hold a general election that could shape the path of Britain forever. For the first time in decades, the choices are between a Conservative Party that has been captured by the hard-right, one that is hell-bent on dragging Britain down the road of nationalist sentiment and driving a wedge between the UK and the European Union, and a truly left-wing Labour Party that could turn the UK away from the Thatcherite road that has led to so much discontentment in pain in Britain.

If you live in Britain, we urge you to get to the polls and vote Labour.
